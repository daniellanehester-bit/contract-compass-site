How to Find Government Contracts You Can Actually Win

"Just check SAM.gov" is the advice everyone gives and almost nobody finds useful. Here's what's actually available, and where each option falls short for a small business trying to win, not just browse.

SAM.gov

The federal system of record. Every federal opportunity above the micro-purchase threshold is supposed to show up here eventually. The problem isn't coverage — it's volume and noise. Thousands of postings, most of which you're not eligible for, with no way to filter specifically by your certification status against the actual requirement.

State procurement portals

Virginia, Texas, Georgia, South Carolina, and every other state runs its own separate system, with its own login, its own search logic, and its own posting cadence. If your business operates in more than one state, or could, you're now checking multiple portals daily just to stay current — and most business owners don't.

Agency-specific portals

Some agencies post opportunities on their own sites before or instead of SAM.gov, especially for smaller purchases. There's no master list of which agencies do this, which means it's mostly discovered by accident or relationship.

Set-aside and small business databases

Useful for confirming your own certification status and understanding the rules, but these are reference tools, not live opportunity feeds. They tell you what you're eligible for in theory, not what's open right now.

Word of mouth and contracting officer relationships

Real, and often how sole-source awards happen — but it's slow to build and doesn't scale. You can't relationship your way to full pipeline coverage across every agency and state you're eligible to bid in.

What actually works

None of these sources is wrong. The problem is doing all of it manually, every day, across every source, while also running your business. That's a full-time research job stacked on top of your actual job.
