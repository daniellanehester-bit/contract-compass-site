How SDVOSB Set-Asides Actually Work (And How to Find Them)

If you're a service-disabled veteran-owned small business, "set-aside" is a word you've probably heard a hundred times without anyone explaining exactly what it does for you. Here's the short version, and where the actual opportunities live.

What SDVOSB set-aside status means

A "set-aside" contract is one the government has decided only certain types of businesses are allowed to bid on. For SDVOSB, that means the contracting officer has determined — under what's known as the "Rule of Two" — that at least two qualified SDVOSB firms are likely to submit competitive offers. When that's true, the contract gets reserved for SDVOSB competition instead of being open to every business in America.

Contracts at or below $250,000 are automatically set aside for small businesses generally, and contracting officers are required to consider SDVOSB and other socioeconomic set-asides before falling back to a general small business set-aside or a sole-source award.

Set-aside vs. sole source — the difference that matters

A set-aside is competitive: multiple SDVOSB firms can bid, and the best proposal wins. A sole-source award is different — the contracting officer awards directly to one SDVOSB firm without a competitive process, usually below a defined dollar threshold, when they don't reasonably expect multiple SDVOSB firms to compete for it. Both matter, but they show up differently: sole-source opportunities are often the result of relationship-building and market research submissions, while set-asides are posted publicly and open to formal bids.

Where these contracts actually get posted

This is where most SDVOSB owners lose the most time. There is no single feed. Federal SDVOSB opportunities post to SAM.gov, but state and local set-asides for veteran-owned businesses are scattered across separate state procurement portals — and each one has its own login, its own search interface, and its own quirks.

The practical result: unless you're checking SAM.gov and every relevant state portal daily, you are missing contracts you are certified to win.

What to actually do about it

  • Keep your SDVOSB certification current — a lapsed cert disqualifies you even from contracts you'd otherwise win.
  • Set NAICS code alerts wherever the portal allows it — but know that alerts still require you to filter noise manually.
  • Check state portals separately from SAM.gov — federal set-asides are only part of the picture.
